What Causes Black Mold Growth?


The sources of some black mold outbreaks are obvious. If your basement floods, or if your building suffers water damage, this can set the foundation for mold growth. However, the origins of some outbreaks are hard to pinpoint.

Black mold requires a warm, moist environment to colonize. Unlike most other mold strains, Stachybotrys needs at least seven days to colonize. As a result, growth sites usually form in out-of-the-way areas, such as basements, behind walls and on top of roofs.

In the outdoors, black mold thrives on plant matter and in the soil. This adaptation allows it to grow on construction materials, which is why it is so problematic in homes and businesses.

It doesn’t take a lot of moisture to allow black mold to grow. Sometimes high humidity levels can lead to an outbreak. This is why dehumidification is often part of the black mold remediation process.

Do You Have a Black Mold Outbreak?


If your black mold outbreak happened because of water damage, then you were probably able to see the growth site. Black mold gets its name from its greenish-black color.

Active sites have a slimy layer on top. If the source of moisture has dissipated, then the mold may appear dry and gray. This, however, does not mean that the mold is harmless; in fact, dried growth sites are more likely to release spores into the air, which can circulate through HVAC systems, cause outbreaks in other rooms, and lead to health complications for building occupants.

Some black mold sites are difficult to identify because they grow in hard-to-see places. If someone complains about persistent allergy symptoms, they may be describing mycotoxin poisoning.

Another telltale sign of mold growth is its musty smell. If you notice an abnormal odor coming from your basement, it could be black mold. Schedule a mold inspection immediately.

Health Risks of Black Mold


The mycotoxins in black mold can be fatal. The initial exposure can cause flu-like symptoms, such as a runny nose, itchy eyes and fatigue, but the symptoms can become severe over time. Prolonged exposure can lead to:

  • Birth defects;

  • Cognitive decline;

  • Internal bleeding;

  • Anxiety;

  • Disorientation;

  • Heart inflammation;

  • And seizures.


This is not a complete list of symptoms. The elderly and infants are at a particularly high risk of suffering severe health complications from mycotoxin poisoning. Exposure can also cause fetal complications in pregnant women.
