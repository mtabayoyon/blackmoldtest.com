Common Causes of Black Mold Outbreaks


Black mold is similar to other species in that it prefers warm, humid areas, but it takes longer to grow and colonize. Some molds require only a few hours or days to form a visible outbreak, but black mold requires up to 12 days. As a result, black mold tends to grow in hard-to-reach, undisturbed areas. Water-damaged basements and ceilings are particularly susceptible, as are burst pipes behind walls.

In nature, Stachybotrys chartarum thrives on plant matter and soil. Its biological properties allow it to grow on construction materials, which is why it can be such a nightmare for business- and homeowners.

Black Mold: Nature’s Silent Killer


Black mold can endanger the lives of you, your family or your customers – especially after prolonged exposure. This requires immediate attention and a professional response.

Some people mistake the symptoms of mycotoxin exposure to typical illnesses. Flu-like symptoms are common; however, these symptoms worsen over time. Other health effects of black mold include:


  • Breathing problems;

  • Hair Loss;

  • Nose bleeds;

  • Bleeding gums;

  • Throat irritation;

  • Runny, stuffy or itchy nose;

  • Bleeding within the lungs;

  • And swelling of the lungs.


Young children and the elderly are at a particularly high risk of contracting these symptoms due to their weak immune systems. However, healthy adults can also fall victim to black mold’s mycotoxins.
